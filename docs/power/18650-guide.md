# 18650 Cell Guide

Everything you need to know about the 18650 lithium-ion cell — the most common rechargeable cell in the world.

---

## Physical Specifications

- **Dimensions:** 18mm diameter x 65mm length (hence "18650")
- **Weight:** 45-50g per cell
- **Nominal voltage:** 3.7V (3.6V on some datasheets — same thing)
- **Full charge voltage:** 4.2V
- **Cutoff voltage:** 2.5V (never go below this)
- **Chemistry:** Lithium-ion (various cathode materials: NMC, NCA, ICR, INR, IMR)

## Top Cells

### High Capacity (Energy Cells)

Best for: long runtime, low-to-moderate drain applications like Meshtastic nodes, flashlights, power banks.

| Cell | Capacity | Max Continuous | Manufacturer | Notes |
|------|----------|---------------|-------------|-------|
| **Samsung 35E** | 3500 mAh | 8A | Samsung SDI | Maximum capacity, low drain only |
| **Panasonic NCR18650B** | 3400 mAh | 6.8A (4.87A per spec) | Panasonic | Legendary cell, used in early Tesla packs |
| **Samsung 30Q** | 3000 mAh | 15A | Samsung SDI | Best balance of capacity and current. Workhorse cell. |
| **LG MJ1** | 3500 mAh | 10A | LG Chem | High capacity with decent current |
| **Sony/Murata VTC6** | 3000 mAh | 15A (20A per some tests) | Murata | Excellent all-rounder |

### High Drain (Power Cells)

Best for: power tools, vaping, high-current applications, motor drivers.

| Cell | Capacity | Max Continuous | Manufacturer | Notes |
|------|----------|---------------|-------------|-------|
| **Samsung 25R** | 2500 mAh | 20A | Samsung SDI | Proven, reliable high-drain cell |
| **LG HG2** | 3000 mAh | 20A | LG Chem | Best high-drain capacity. "Chocolate" cell. |
| **Sony/Murata VTC5A** | 2600 mAh | 25A | Murata | Very high drain |
| **Samsung 20S** | 2000 mAh | 30A | Samsung SDI | Extreme drain, lower capacity |
| **Molicel P26A** | 2600 mAh | 25A | Molicel | Newer, excellent performance |

### The Capacity vs Drain Tradeoff

This is a fundamental tradeoff in lithium-ion cell design:

```
More capacity (mAh) ←————————→ More current (A)
   3500mAh / 8A                    2000mAh / 30A
```

You cannot have both maximum capacity and maximum current in the same cell. The internal chemistry and electrode design that enables high current limits energy storage, and vice versa.

**For Meshtastic and most electronics projects:** choose a high-capacity cell. The Samsung 30Q (3000mAh, 15A) or LG HG2 (3000mAh, 20A) are ideal — more than enough current for any microcontroller project with excellent capacity.

## Flat Top vs Button Top

| Feature | Flat Top | Button Top |
|---------|----------|------------|
| Appearance | Flat positive terminal | Small raised bump on positive terminal |
| Length | 65mm standard | ~66-67mm (slightly longer due to button) |
| Use case | Battery packs (spot welded) | Devices with spring contacts, flashlights |
| Compatibility | May not work in devices that need the button to make contact | Works in almost everything |

**For T-Beam and most dev boards:** either works, but check your specific holder. Most 18650 holders with spring contacts accept both.

## Protected vs Unprotected

### Unprotected Cells
- Raw cell with no built-in protection circuitry
- Slightly shorter (65mm)
- Higher current capability (no protection circuit in the current path)
- **Requires external protection** — a BMS, protection board, or a device with built-in low-voltage cutoff
- What the manufacturer actually makes and sells to OEMs

### Protected Cells
- A standard cell with a small PCB (protection circuit module) attached to one end
- PCB provides: overcharge protection, over-discharge protection, short circuit protection, overcurrent protection
- Slightly longer (~68-69mm) and slightly wider — may not fit in tight holders
- Lower maximum current (protection circuit has its own current limit, typically 7-10A)
- More expensive

### Which to Buy?

- **For Meshtastic T-Beam:** unprotected is fine — the T-Beam's AXP PMU handles battery management. Protected cells may be too long for some T-Beam holders.
- **For flashlights without built-in protection:** use protected cells.
- **For custom battery packs with a BMS:** use unprotected cells.
- **When in doubt:** protected cells are safer for general use.

## Authentic vs Counterfeit

The 18650 counterfeit market is massive. Fake cells with inflated specs are everywhere, especially on Amazon (third-party sellers), eBay, and AliExpress.

### Red Flags — Almost Certainly Fake
- Any cell claiming over 3600mAh capacity (no 18650 exceeds this as of 2025)
- "UltraFire" or "SureFire" branded 18650s claiming 4000-9900mAh — these are universally garbage, typically rewrapped 500-1000mAh cells
- Impossibly cheap prices (real Samsung 30Q costs $3-5 per cell from reputable sources)
- Cells that look right but feel too light (fakes often use cheaper, lighter internals)

### How to Verify
1. **Buy from reputable sources only** (see below)
2. **Weigh the cell** — real high-capacity cells weigh 45-48g. Fakes are often 30-38g.
3. **Test capacity** — charge to 4.2V, discharge at 500mA to 2.5V, measure mAh. Use a charger with capacity testing (XTAR VC4SL, LiitoKala Lii-500) or a dedicated tester (ZB2L3).
4. **Check the wrap** — authentic cells have clear, consistent printing. Fakes often have blurry text or slightly wrong colors.

### Reputable Sources (US)
- **18650batterystore.com** — excellent reputation, fast shipping
- **IMRbatteries.com** — long-standing trusted source
- **Liion Wholesale (liionwholesale.com)** — bulk pricing, tested cells
- **Illumn.com** — curated selection, good for flashlight cells

### Reputable Sources (International)

- **Fogstar** — UK

**Never buy 18650 cells from random Amazon marketplace sellers, eBay, or wish.com.**

## Charging

### Dedicated Chargers

Always use a proper charger with CC-CV charging profile and automatic termination.

| Charger | Bays | Features | Price Range |
|---------|------|----------|------------|
| **XTAR VC4SL** | 4 | Capacity test, internal resistance, USB-C input, very accurate | $25-35 |
| **XTAR VC4** | 4 | Capacity test, good all-rounder | $20-25 |
| **Nitecore SC4** | 4 | Fast charging up to 3A/slot, digital display | $30-40 |
| **LiitoKala Lii-500** | 4 | Capacity testing, budget option | $15-20 |
| **XTAR MC2** | 2 | Tiny, USB-powered, travel charger | $8-12 |

### Charging Parameters
- **Charge current:** 0.5C is standard (1.5A for a 3000mAh cell). 1C (3A) is the maximum for most cells. Slower charging = longer cell life.
- **Charge voltage:** 4.20V +/- 0.05V. The charger handles this — but verify with a multimeter when you first get a new charger.
- **Termination:** current drops to ~C/20 (50-100mA). Charger should stop and indicate "full."
- **Never leave cells in a hot charger indefinitely** — remove them once charged.

### In-Device Charging
- T-Beam charges its 18650 through USB via the AXP2101 PMU — this is fine for topping off
- Heltec V3 charges its LiPo through USB — also fine
- For fastest and healthiest charging, use a dedicated charger

## Holders and Pack Building

### Single Cell Holders
- **Battery sled/holder with leads:** snap-in holder with positive and negative wire leads. Good for prototyping.
- **Spring-contact holders:** like flashlight tubes. Accept both flat and button top.
- **PCB mount holders:** Keystone 1042/1043 series. Solder to a PCB.

### Multi-Cell Pack Building
- **Spot welding nickel strips:** the proper way to build packs. Uses a spot welder to attach nickel strips to cell terminals.
  - Pure nickel strip: 0.15mm x 8mm typical for moderate current (5-10A)
  - 0.2mm strip for higher current
  - **Never solder directly to 18650 cells** — the heat can damage the cell and create a weak joint. Spot welding is fast enough to avoid heat damage.
- **Spot welders:** kWeld (best DIY option, ~$70 kit), Malectrics, or cheap Amazon spot welders (inconsistent quality but works for small packs)
- **Insulation:** use fish paper or adhesive cardboard rings between cells and on exposed terminals. A cell-to-cell short in a pack can be catastrophic.
- **Configuration:** always use matched cells (same model, same batch, tested capacity within 50mAh)

### Battery Sleds on Dev Boards
- **T-Beam:** has a built-in 18650 holder on the bottom of the board
  - **Battery orientation: the positive terminal faces toward the USB port** on most T-Beam versions — but always verify with the silkscreen markings on your specific board before inserting
  - Wrong polarity can damage the board permanently
- **DIY:** 18650 holder + TP4056 module + boost converter = simple power system for any project

## Safety and Storage

### Safety Rules
1. **Never carry loose 18650s in a pocket or bag** with metal objects (keys, coins, other batteries). A short circuit across the terminals can cause the cell to rapidly heat, vent, and potentially catch fire. Use a plastic case.
2. **Inspect cell wraps regularly.** If the wrap is torn or nicked, the entire metal body of the cell is the negative terminal — any contact with the exposed can to the positive terminal is a short circuit. Rewrap damaged cells with heat-shrink tube (standard 18650 wraps are available cheaply).
3. **Never puncture, crush, or deform a cell.**
4. **Never throw 18650s in the trash.** Recycle at battery recycling points (Home Depot, Lowes, Best Buy, Call2Recycle locations in the US).
5. **Use a fireproof container for storage.** LiPo safety bags, ammo cans (with the seal removed so gas can vent), or a metal container on a non-flammable surface.
6. **If a cell gets hot during charging or use, or shows any signs of swelling, stop using it immediately.** Place it on a non-flammable surface away from anything burnable and let it cool. Dispose of it.

### Storage
- Store at **3.7-3.8V** (about 40-50% charge) for long-term storage
- Store in a **cool, dry place** — room temperature is fine, a cool basement is better
- **Never store at full charge** (4.2V) for extended periods — accelerates aging
- **Never store fully depleted** — self-discharge can push the cell below safe voltage
- **Check stored cells every 3-6 months** — top up to 3.7V if they've dropped
- Store in **plastic cell cases** (available for $1-2 for a 2-cell or 4-cell case)

### Cell Cases
Cheap plastic cases that hold 2 or 4 cells are essential. Buy a bunch. They:
- Prevent shorts from accidental contact with metal
- Keep cells organized
- Protect wraps from tearing
- Available everywhere: Amazon, battery stores, flashlight retailers
- Cost: $0.50-$2 each
