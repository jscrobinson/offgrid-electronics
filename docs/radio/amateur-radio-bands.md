# Amateur Radio Band Plans

## US Amateur Radio License Classes

| Class | Privileges | Exam |
|-------|-----------|------|
| **Technician** | All amateur privileges above 30 MHz (VHF/UHF). Limited HF on 80m, 40m, 15m, 10m (CW only on some; voice/data on 10m). | 35-question multiple choice |
| **General** | Technician privileges + most HF bands with significant voice/data segments. | Technician + 35-question multiple choice |
| **Extra** | All amateur frequencies and modes. Full access to all band segments. | Technician + General + 50-question multiple choice |

License term: 10 years, renewable. No Morse code requirement for any class since 2007. FCC license fee: $35 (as of 2024). Volunteer Examiner (VE) session fee varies.

## HF Bands

### 160 Meters (1.800 - 2.000 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: "Top Band." Ground wave and nighttime sky wave propagation. Very long antennas required (full dipole ~260 feet). High atmospheric noise.
- **Best time**: Nighttime, especially winter.
- **Propagation**: Ground wave (local), sky wave at night (regional to continental).
- **License**: General and Extra.

### 80 Meters (3.500 - 4.000 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: Reliable nighttime regional band. High noise in summer (thunderstorms). Good for nets and ragchewing within a few hundred to 1000+ miles at night.
- **Best time**: Nighttime year-round. Daytime range is limited (~100-200 miles).
- **Propagation**: Ground wave (daytime), sky wave (nighttime, regional).
- **Band plan**:
  - 3.500 - 3.600 MHz: CW (Extra below 3.525)
  - 3.600 - 3.700 MHz: RTTY/Data
  - 3.700 - 3.800 MHz: SSB (Extra only)
  - 3.800 - 4.000 MHz: SSB (General and Extra)
- **Common frequencies**: 3.860 MHz (SSB net frequency)

### 60 Meters (5.330 - 5.410 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: Channelized. Five specific channels. Shared with government. USB only, 100W ERP max.
- **Channels**: 5.332, 5.348, 5.358.5, 5.373, 5.405 MHz (center frequencies)
- **License**: General and Extra.

### 40 Meters (7.000 - 7.300 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: The workhorse band. Reliable day and night. Daytime: regional (several hundred miles). Nighttime: continental to worldwide.
- **Best time**: Almost any time. Daytime for regional, nighttime for DX.
- **Propagation**: Sky wave. D-layer absorption limits daytime range. Excellent nighttime propagation.
- **Band plan**:
  - 7.000 - 7.025 MHz: CW (Extra only)
  - 7.025 - 7.125 MHz: CW (General and Extra)
  - 7.125 - 7.175 MHz: SSB (General and Extra)
  - 7.175 - 7.300 MHz: SSB (General and Extra)
- **Common frequencies**: 7.200 MHz area (SSB), 7.030 MHz (QRP CW calling)

### 30 Meters (10.100 - 10.150 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: Narrow band, CW and digital modes only. No voice. Shared with government (secondary allocation). 200W max.
- **Best time**: Day and night. Excellent propagation characteristics.
- **License**: General and Extra.

### 20 Meters (14.000 - 14.350 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: The premiere DX (long distance) band. Open to worldwide propagation during daylight hours, especially during moderate to high solar activity. The most popular HF band.
- **Best time**: Daytime. May stay open into early evening. During solar maximum, can be open nearly 24 hours.
- **Propagation**: Sky wave. Single or multi-hop. Worldwide.
- **Band plan**:
  - 14.000 - 14.025 MHz: CW (Extra only)
  - 14.025 - 14.150 MHz: CW (General and Extra)
  - 14.150 - 14.175 MHz: SSB (Extra only)
  - 14.175 - 14.225 MHz: SSB (General and Extra, phone segment starts here for General)
  - 14.225 - 14.350 MHz: SSB (General and Extra)
- **Common frequencies**: 14.070 MHz (PSK31/FT8 digital), 14.300 MHz (emergency/maritime net)

### 17 Meters (18.068 - 18.168 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: WARC band (no contests allowed). Good DX band, similar to 20m but less crowded.
- **Best time**: Daytime.
- **License**: General and Extra.

### 15 Meters (21.000 - 21.450 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: Excellent DX band during solar maximum. Can be completely dead during solar minimum. When open, worldwide propagation.
- **Best time**: Daytime during moderate-to-high solar activity.
- **Propagation**: Sky wave. Highly dependent on solar cycle.
- **Band plan**:
  - 21.000 - 21.025 MHz: CW (Extra only)
  - 21.025 - 21.200 MHz: CW (General and Extra)
  - 21.200 - 21.225 MHz: SSB (Extra only)
  - 21.225 - 21.275 MHz: SSB (General and Extra)
  - 21.275 - 21.450 MHz: SSB (General and Extra)
- **Technician**: CW privileges 21.025 - 21.200 MHz.
- **Common frequencies**: 21.070 MHz (digital)

### 12 Meters (24.890 - 24.990 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: WARC band (no contests). DX band dependent on solar cycle.
- **License**: General and Extra.

### 10 Meters (28.000 - 29.700 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: Lowest HF band still capable of sky wave propagation, but only during high solar activity or sporadic-E events. When open, worldwide DX with modest antennas and power. Can be dead for years during solar minimum. Also supports local FM repeaters in some areas.
- **Best time**: Daytime during solar maximum. Sporadic-E openings in summer (unpredictable, exciting).
- **Propagation**: Sky wave (solar dependent), sporadic-E, line-of-sight at higher sub-bands.
- **Band plan**:
  - 28.000 - 28.070 MHz: CW
  - 28.070 - 28.150 MHz: RTTY/Data
  - 28.150 - 28.300 MHz: CW/SSB (beacons at 28.190-28.225)
  - 28.300 - 29.300 MHz: SSB
  - 29.000 - 29.200 MHz: AM
  - 29.300 - 29.510 MHz: Satellite
  - 29.520 - 29.700 MHz: FM (29.600 FM calling frequency)
- **Technician**: Full privileges 28.000 - 28.500 MHz (CW, SSB, digital). All modes 28.300 - 28.500 MHz.
- **Common frequencies**: 28.400 MHz (SSB calling), 29.600 MHz (FM calling)

## VHF Bands

### 6 Meters (50 - 54 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: "The Magic Band." Normally line-of-sight, but subject to dramatic sporadic-E openings that can provide sky wave propagation across continents. Tropospheric ducting also possible.
- **Propagation**: Line-of-sight, sporadic-E (summer), tropospheric ducting.
- **Band plan**:
  - 50.000 - 50.100 MHz: CW (50.060 beacon sub-band)
  - 50.100 - 50.300 MHz: SSB (50.125 SSB calling frequency)
  - 50.300 - 50.600 MHz: Digital/experimental
  - 51.000 - 54.000 MHz: FM (52.525 FM calling frequency)
- **License**: Technician and above (all modes).
- **Common frequencies**: 50.125 MHz (SSB calling), 52.525 MHz (FM calling)

### 2 Meters (144 - 148 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: The most popular amateur band. Dense repeater coverage in populated areas. Primary band for local and regional communication. Reliable line-of-sight propagation. Handheld radios are practical.
- **Propagation**: Line-of-sight. Repeaters extend range to 50-100+ miles. Occasional tropospheric ducting, meteor scatter.
- **Band plan**:
  - 144.000 - 144.100 MHz: CW/SSB (weak signal)
  - 144.100 - 144.200 MHz: CW/SSB (144.200 SSB calling)
  - 144.200 - 144.275 MHz: SSB
  - 144.390 MHz: APRS (Automatic Packet Reporting System, North America)
  - 144.300 - 144.500 MHz: Satellite, new modes
  - 145.200 - 145.500 MHz: Repeater outputs (some areas)
  - 146.010 - 146.400 MHz: Repeater inputs
  - 146.400 - 146.600 MHz: Simplex
  - **146.520 MHz: National simplex calling frequency**
  - 146.610 - 147.000 MHz: Repeater outputs
  - 147.000 - 147.400 MHz: Repeater outputs
  - 147.400 - 147.600 MHz: Simplex
  - 147.600 - 148.000 MHz: Repeater inputs
- **Repeater offset**: +/- 600 kHz (standard)
- **License**: Technician and above.
- **Common frequencies**:
  - 146.520 MHz — National simplex calling frequency
  - 144.200 MHz — SSB calling frequency
  - 144.390 MHz — APRS
  - 145.050 MHz — Satellite uplink (common)
  - 146.580/146.460 MHz — Common simplex

### 1.25 Meters (222 - 225 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: Less crowded than 2m. Some repeater activity. Limited equipment availability.
- **License**: Technician and above.
- **Common frequencies**: 223.500 MHz (simplex calling)

## UHF Bands

### 70 Centimeters (420 - 450 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: Second most popular band after 2 meters. More repeaters being added. Signals penetrate buildings slightly better than 2m in some situations but overall shorter range. Better for indoor/urban use in some cases. Shared with government radar (secondary allocation in many areas).
- **Propagation**: Line-of-sight. Shorter range than 2m for equivalent power and antenna height.
- **Band plan**:
  - 420.000 - 432.000 MHz: Mixed, ATV (amateur television)
  - 432.000 - 432.100 MHz: Weak signal CW/SSB
  - 432.100 MHz: SSB calling frequency
  - 433.000 - 435.000 MHz: Auxiliary links, repeater links
  - 435.000 - 438.000 MHz: Satellite
  - 440.000 - 445.000 MHz: Repeater outputs (some areas)
  - **446.000 MHz: National simplex calling frequency**
  - 445.000 - 450.000 MHz: Repeater inputs (some areas)
- **Repeater offset**: +/- 5 MHz (standard)
- **License**: Technician and above.
- **Common frequencies**:
  - 446.000 MHz — National simplex calling frequency
  - 432.100 MHz — SSB calling frequency

### 33 Centimeters (902 - 928 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: Shared with ISM band. Limited activity. Good for experimentation.
- **License**: Technician and above.
- **Common frequencies**: 927.500 MHz (simplex calling)

### 23 Centimeters (1240 - 1300 MHz)

- **Characteristics**: Microwave amateur band. Used for ATV, digital experimentation, satellite, moonbounce.
- **License**: Technician and above.

## Band Characteristics Summary

| Band | Best For | Propagation | Time of Day | Antenna Size |
|------|----------|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| 80m | Regional nets, ragchewing | Ground/sky wave | Night | Large (~130 ft dipole) |
| 40m | Regional/continental | Sky wave | Day and night | Moderate (~66 ft dipole) |
| 20m | Worldwide DX | Sky wave | Day | Moderate (~33 ft dipole) |
| 15m | Worldwide DX (solar max) | Sky wave | Day (solar dependent) | Smaller (~22 ft dipole) |
| 10m | DX, local FM | Sky wave/sporadic-E | Day (solar dependent) | Small (~16 ft dipole) |
| 6m | DX openings, local | LOS/sporadic-E | Variable | ~10 ft dipole |
| 2m | Local/regional, repeaters | Line of sight | Anytime | ~39 in (1m) whip |
| 70cm | Local, repeaters, urban | Line of sight | Anytime | ~17 in (43cm) whip |

## Solar Cycle and HF Propagation

The Sun's 11-year sunspot cycle strongly affects HF propagation:

- **Solar maximum**: Higher bands (10m, 12m, 15m, 17m) are frequently open for worldwide DX. 20m open nearly 24 hours.
- **Solar minimum**: Higher bands may be dead for months. 40m and 80m become the primary HF bands. 20m still opens during the day but closes earlier.
- **Solar Flux Index (SFI)**: Measured daily. Higher = better HF propagation on higher bands. Values range from ~65 (minimum) to 200+ (maximum).
- **K-index and A-index**: Measure geomagnetic disturbance. Lower = better propagation. K > 4 or A > 15 often means degraded HF conditions.

Resources for propagation:
- NOAA Space Weather Prediction Center: www.swpc.noaa.gov
- DX clusters and propagation beacons
- VOACAP propagation prediction software
