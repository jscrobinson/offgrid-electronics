# Q-Codes Reference

## About Q-Codes

Q-codes are three-letter codes beginning with "Q" developed for radiotelegraph (CW/Morse code) communication to save time and overcome language barriers. Each code has two meanings: one when used as a **question** (with a question mark) and one when used as a **statement/answer**.

While originally for CW, many Q-codes are now commonly used in voice (phone) communication on amateur radio, especially QTH, QSL, QSY, QRM, QRN, QRP, QRO, QSO, and QRZ.

---

## Most Common Q-Codes

### QRA — Station Name
- **Question**: What is the name of your station?
- **Statement**: The name of my station is _____.

### QRG — Frequency
- **Question**: What is my exact frequency?
- **Statement**: Your exact frequency is _____ kHz/MHz.

### QRH — Frequency Variation
- **Question**: Does my frequency vary?
- **Statement**: Your frequency varies.

### QRI — Tone Quality
- **Question**: How is the tone of my transmission?
- **Statement**: The tone of your transmission is (1=good, 2=variable, 3=bad).

### QRK — Readability
- **Question**: What is the readability of my signals?
- **Statement**: The readability of your signals is (1-5, where 5=perfect).

### QRL — Frequency in Use
- **Question**: Is this frequency in use? (Very common on CW. Sent before calling CQ.)
- **Statement**: This frequency is in use. Please do not interfere.
- **Usage**: Before transmitting CQ on a CW frequency, send "QRL?" and listen for a response. If you hear "QRL" or "C" (yes), move to another frequency.

### QRM — Man-Made Interference
- **Question**: Are you being interfered with?
- **Statement**: I am being interfered with. (1=nil, 2=slightly, 3=moderately, 4=severely, 5=extremely)
- **Common voice usage**: "I'm getting a lot of QRM" = there is interference from other stations or electronic devices.

### QRN — Natural Interference (Static)
- **Question**: Are you troubled by static?
- **Statement**: I am troubled by static. (1-5 scale)
- **Common voice usage**: "There's heavy QRN tonight" = atmospheric noise/static is bad.

### QRO — High Power / Increase Power
- **Question**: Shall I increase power?
- **Statement**: Increase power. / I am running high power.
- **Common voice usage**: "I'm running QRO" = I'm using high power (typically 100W+).

### QRP — Low Power / Decrease Power
- **Question**: Shall I decrease power?
- **Statement**: Decrease power. / I am running low power.
- **Common voice usage**: "I'm running QRP" = I am using low power (generally 5 watts or less on CW, 10W on SSB). QRP operation is a popular sub-hobby within amateur radio.

### QRQ — Send Faster
- **Question**: Shall I send faster?
- **Statement**: Send faster (_____ WPM).

### QRS — Send Slower
- **Question**: Shall I send more slowly?
- **Statement**: Send more slowly (_____ WPM).
- **Common voice usage**: "QRS please" = please slow down (used on CW).

### QRT — Stop Transmitting
- **Question**: Shall I stop transmitting?
- **Statement**: I am stopping transmitting. / Stop transmitting.
- **Common voice usage**: "I'm going QRT" = I'm shutting down the station / going off the air.

### QRU — Nothing Further
- **Question**: Have you anything for me?
- **Statement**: I have nothing for you.

### QRV — Ready
- **Question**: Are you ready?
- **Statement**: I am ready.

### QRX — Stand By
- **Question**: When will you call again?
- **Statement**: I will call you again at _____ (time). / Stand by, I will call you.
- **Common voice usage**: "QRX one minute" = stand by for one minute.

### QRZ — Who Is Calling Me?
- **Question**: Who is calling me?
- **Statement**: You are being called by _____.
- **Common voice usage**: "QRZ?" = "Who is calling?" Used when you hear someone call but didn't catch their callsign. Also the name of the popular amateur radio callsign database (QRZ.com).

### QSA — Signal Strength
- **Question**: What is my signal strength?
- **Statement**: Your signal strength is (1-5).

### QSB — Fading
- **Question**: Are my signals fading?
- **Statement**: Your signals are fading.
- **Common voice usage**: "Your signal has some QSB" = your signal is fading in and out.

### QSD — Keying Defects
- **Question**: Is my keying defective?
- **Statement**: Your keying is defective.

### QSK — Break-In Keying
- **Question**: Can you hear me between your signals?
- **Statement**: I can hear you between my signals. (Full break-in CW capability.)

### QSL — Acknowledge / Confirm
- **Question**: Can you acknowledge receipt?
- **Statement**: I acknowledge receipt. / I confirm.
- **Common voice usage**: "QSL" = confirmed, understood, acknowledged. Also: "Can I get a QSL card?" — a printed postcard confirming a radio contact, collected by amateur operators.

### QSO — Contact / Communication
- **Question**: Can you communicate with _____ directly?
- **Statement**: I can communicate with _____ directly.
- **Common voice usage**: "Nice QSO" = nice conversation/contact. A "QSO" is the standard term for a completed radio contact between two or more stations.

### QSP — Relay
- **Question**: Will you relay to _____?
- **Statement**: I will relay to _____.

### QST — General Call
- **Statement**: General call to all stations. (Used for announcements, not for individual contacts.)

### QSX — Listening On
- **Question**: Will you listen on _____ frequency?
- **Statement**: I am listening on _____ frequency.
- **Usage**: "QSX 14.205" = I am listening on 14.205 MHz (used in split-frequency operation).

### QSY — Change Frequency
- **Question**: Shall I change frequency?
- **Statement**: Change frequency to _____.
- **Common voice usage**: "Let's QSY to 146.55" = let's move to 146.55 MHz.

### QTC — Message Traffic
- **Question**: How many messages do you have?
- **Statement**: I have _____ messages for you.

### QTH — Location
- **Question**: What is your position/location?
- **Statement**: My position/location is _____.
- **Common voice usage**: "My QTH is Denver, Colorado" = I am located in Denver, Colorado.

### QTR — Time
- **Question**: What is the correct time?
- **Statement**: The correct time is _____.

## Notes

1. On CW, Q-codes save significant time — three letters versus a full phrase.
2. On voice, the most commonly used Q-codes are: QTH, QSL, QSO, QSY, QRM, QRN, QRP, QRO, QRZ, QRT, QRX, QSB.
3. Avoid overusing Q-codes on voice when plain language would be clearer. "What is your location?" is fine on voice. Q-codes on voice are conventions, not requirements.
4. Q-codes are also used in some non-amateur contexts (maritime, aviation, military) but usage and meanings may vary slightly.
5. The full list of Q-codes contains hundreds of entries. This reference covers the ones most commonly encountered in amateur radio.
