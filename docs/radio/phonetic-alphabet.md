# Phonetic Alphabets

## NATO / ICAO Phonetic Alphabet

The standard phonetic alphabet used internationally in aviation, maritime, amateur radio, military, and emergency communications. Adopted by NATO and the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO).

### Numbers

| Number | Spoken As | Pronunciation |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| 0 | Zero | ZEE-RO |
| 1 | One | WUN |
| 2 | Two | TOO |
| 3 | Three | TREE |
| 4 | Four | FOW-ER |
| 5 | Five | FIFE |
| 6 | Six | SIX |
| 7 | Seven | SEV-EN |
| 8 | Eight | AIT |
| 9 | Niner | NINE-ER |

**Note**: "Niner" is used instead of "nine" to avoid confusion with "nein" (German for "no"). "Tree" instead of "three" and "fife" instead of "five" improve clarity over poor radio links.

### Special Numbers

| Expression | Spoken As |
|-----------|-----------|
| 100 | One hundred |
| 1000 | One thousand (or "one tousand") |
| Decimal point | Day-see-mal |

## Usage Examples

### Spelling a Callsign

Callsign: **W1ABC**

Spoken: "Whiskey One Alpha Bravo Charlie"

### Spelling a Word

Word: **HELP**

Spoken: "Hotel Echo Lima Papa"

### Giving a Frequency

Frequency: **146.520 MHz**

Spoken: "One four six decimal five two zero megahertz"

Or more formally: "Wun fow-er six day-see-mal fife too zee-ro"

### Giving Grid Square / Location Code

Grid: **FN31**

Spoken: "Foxtrot November tree wun"

## Tips for Clear Voice Communication

1. **Speak slowly and clearly.** Do not rush phonetic spelling.
2. **Key up, pause briefly, then speak.** This prevents clipping the first syllable.
3. **Use phonetics for any word or callsign that might be misheard.** Even common words can be garbled over a noisy radio link.
4. **Spell critical information phonetically**: Names, callsigns, grid squares, addresses, serial numbers, medication names.
5. **Use "I say again" before repeating important information**, not "repeat" (which in military usage means "fire again").
6. **Say "figures" before a group of numbers** to alert the listener: "Figures, wun too tree fow-er fife."
7. **Emphasize syllables** as shown in the pronunciation guide. The stressed syllable should be louder and more distinct.
8. **On amateur radio**, always identify with your callsign using phonetics at least at the beginning and end of a contact, and every 10 minutes during a long conversation.
